05 — Responsible AI

AI Reflection

A reflective acknowledgement of how AI tools were used — and not used — in this project, following Stage 2 Responsible AI expectations.

Overview

This page documents my use of Generative AI tools throughout the A2 Research and Proposal task. It is structured around the four pillars of the Responsible AI Use Framework and reflects Stage 2 expectations: Skill Expansion → Critical Autonomy.

AI tools were used selectively and intentionally — as a thinking support, not as a replacement for my own research, analysis, or design judgement. All design decisions documented in this website are my own, informed and verified through research, course materials, and critical reflection.

Declaration

AI tools used: Claude (Anthropic) — used for drafting, clarifying, and exploring alternatives during the writing process. All AI-assisted outputs were critically reviewed, substantially revised, and verified against research sources before inclusion.

Reflection: Four Pillars

My reflection is structured around the four pillars of the Responsible AI Use Framework applied in this course.

01

Human-Centred Ethical Judgement

The community I chose — South Asian diaspora in Australia — includes people with diverse and sometimes contested cultural identities. I was aware from the start that AI tools trained predominantly on Western data could generate culturally reductive or stereotyped suggestions about this community. When Claude generated early descriptions of South Asian culture, several relied on familiar tropes (food, festivals, Bollywood). I rejected these and replaced them with language grounded in my own understanding, user interview data, and FECCA reports. Ethical vigilance was needed not just in what was included, but in how the community was framed linguistically throughout.

02

Socio-Technical Agency — Intentional Use

I chose to use AI for specific, bounded tasks: exploring alternative phrasings, generating scaffold text for heavily factual sections, and double-checking logical consistency across requirements. I deliberately chose not to use AI for: community research (interviews and precedent analysis require direct human engagement); persona development (which depends on real data from real people); and the design system's visual decisions (where my own aesthetic judgement, grounded in cultural knowledge, was required). The decision about when and where AI was appropriate was made consciously and revised as the project evolved — not applied uniformly.

03

Verification & Critical AI Inquiry

Every AI-assisted output was verified before use. The most significant verification was in the precedent analysis: Claude's description of the MOSAIC portal incorrectly stated it offered multilingual content. I discovered this through direct inspection of the site. This reinforced a lesson I already suspected — AI tools hallucinate plausible-sounding facts, especially for niche or Australian-specific platforms that may be underrepresented in training data. I adopted a policy of direct-source verification for any specific factual claim. I also used the WCAG Contrast Checker independently to verify all colour ratios, not relying on Claude's stated values alone.

04

Transparency & Responsible Method

This reflection page itself represents my commitment to transparency. I have documented not only where AI was used, but where it was not, and why. I have acknowledged the errors AI produced and the corrections I made. The decisions in this project — the choice of community, the design requirements, the information architecture, the design system choices — all reflect my own judgement, developed through engagement with course materials, studio activities, and independent research. AI helped me articulate and refine; it did not decide.

What I Learned

Engaging with AI tools in a structured and reflective way taught me several things I did not fully appreciate before this task:

→

AI tools encode cultural assumptions

The cultural stereotyping I encountered in early AI-generated content was not an aberration — it reflects the training data these models learn from. Working with communities that are underrepresented in Western internet content requires extra critical vigilance, not just a quick read-through of AI output.

→

Verification requires domain knowledge

To identify the MOSAIC error, I needed to already know what to look for — which meant I needed prior research to verify AI claims about research. AI cannot substitute for the baseline knowledge that makes verification possible.

→

AI is most useful when the task is bounded

Using AI to explore three alternative phrasings of a research question was efficient and generative. Asking AI to "write the research section" would have produced generic content disconnected from my actual findings. Bounded, specific prompts produced better scaffolds than open-ended ones.
