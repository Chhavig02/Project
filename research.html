01 — Practice-Based Research

Research

Investigating the South Asian diaspora community in Australia — their needs, experiences, and the design opportunities that emerge from their stories.

Research Framing

The research was framed around a central question: What does the South Asian diaspora community in Australia need from a community-focused digital platform, and what barriers currently prevent those needs from being met?

This framing was drawn directly from the CCI brief's emphasis on social cohesion, representation, and accessibility. The South Asian diaspora was chosen as the community focus because it is one of the fastest-growing population groups in Australia, yet is often treated as a monolith, obscuring its internal diversity and varied needs.

Research Focus

Community representation · Digital access · Identity and belonging · Cultural continuity · Intergenerational tensions

Research Approach & Methods

A practice-based, mixed-methods research approach was adopted, drawing on three complementary methods to triangulate findings:

01

Desk Research & Secondary Sources

Review of ABS 2021 Census data on the South Asian-born population in Australia; academic literature on diaspora identity, digital community building, and cultural accessibility in web design; and reports from organisations such as the Multicultural Council of Australia and FECCA.

Quantitative + Qualitative
02

Precedent Analysis

Analysis of three existing community platforms: the Indian Link Australia website, the MOSAIC multicultural community portal, and the South Asian Arts UK website. Evaluation focused on information architecture, accessibility, tone of voice, cultural representation, and identified gaps.

Comparative Analysis
03

User Interviews (Informal)

Informal structured conversations with three members of the South Asian diaspora in Australia (ages 22, 38, and 64), representing second-generation, 1.5-generation, and first-generation migrants respectively. Conversations focused on community connection, digital habits, and unmet needs.

Precedent Insight

Existing platforms either treat the South Asian community as an audience to inform (news sites) or as service recipients (government portals). None create a genuine sense of digital ownership, belonging, or cross-generational storytelling.

User Research Findings

A

Ananya, 22

Second-generation · Sydney

"I feel Australian but I'm always asked where I'm from. I want a space that doesn't make me choose between identities."

  • Representation that reflects hyphenated identity
  • Stories from people who look like her
  • Mobile-first access (primarily phone user)
  • Content that isn't just about food and festivals
R

Rajiv, 38

1.5-generation · Melbourne

"I want my kids to know where we come from, but also feel proud of being Australian. I can't find anything that holds both."

  • Intergenerational storytelling tools
  • Community event discovery
  • Resource guides for newcomers (extended family)
  • Cultural heritage content in accessible English
S

Sunita, 64

First-generation · Brisbane

"I find these websites very confusing. Too many things. I just want to find my community and feel welcomed."

  • Simple, uncluttered navigation
  • Large text and high contrast
  • Familiar cultural signals (imagery, language)
  • Community directory and local event listings

Key Findings & Insights

Five key insights emerged from across the research methods. These insights directly inform the design requirements on the next page.

F1

Identity is multi-layered and contested

South Asian diaspora members in Australia do not experience a single, unified identity. First-generation migrants prioritise cultural continuity; second-generation users resist simplistic cultural labelling. Any platform must accommodate spectrum identities rather than presenting a singular "South Asian" voice.

Source: User interviews, academic literature (Vertovec 2009)
F2

Accessibility barriers exclude older community members

Existing platforms consistently fail older diaspora members through poor contrast ratios, small text, dense navigation, and assumed digital literacy. This is both a UX failure and an ethical failure — it excludes the very generation most invested in cultural preservation.

Source: Precedent analysis, Sunita interview
F3

Community ownership of content is absent from existing platforms

All three platforms analysed treat the community as a passive audience. None allow community members to contribute stories, share experiences, or shape the narrative. This creates platforms about communities rather than for and by them.

Source: Precedent analysis
F4

Mobile access is the primary mode for younger users

All three interview participants under 40 reported using their phone as their primary internet access device. Any platform must be designed mobile-first, not as a desktop site with mobile adaptation.

Source: User interviews, ABS digital access data
F5

Trust and cultural safety are prerequisite to engagement

Community members are hesitant to engage with platforms that feel corporate, governmental, or designed without cultural understanding. Tone, visual identity, and content framing are not cosmetic — they determine whether users feel safe, seen, and welcome.

Source: User interviews, FECCA 2022 report
